🧮 Zeller’s Congruence Explained

Zeller’s Congruence is a brilliant mathematical formula used to determine the day of the week for any date. The version used here is optimized for the Gregorian calendar.

h = ( q + ⌊ 13 ( m + 1 ) 5 ⌋ + K + ⌊ K 4 ⌋ + ⌊ J 4 ⌋ + 5 J )   mod   7 h = \left( q + \left\lfloor \frac{13(m + 1)}{5} \right\rfloor + K + \left\lfloor \frac{K}{4} \right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac{J}{4} \right\rfloor + 5J \right) \bmod 7

Or

h = (q + ⌊13(m+1)/5⌋ + K + ⌊K/4⌋ + ⌊J/4⌋ + 5J) mod 7
  • h = The day of the week (0 = Saturday, 1 = Sunday, ..., 6 = Friday)
  • q = The day of the month
  • m = The month (3 = March, ..., 12 = December). For Jan & Feb, m is 13 & 14 of the previous year.
  • K = The year of the century (year % 100)
  • J = The zero-based century (Math.floor(year / 100))

Historical Adjustment Note:

The Gregorian calendar was adopted in October 1582, which involved skipping 10 days (from October 5th to 14th) to realign the calendar with the seasons. This app correctly identifies these non-existent dates.

🤔 Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is Zeller’s Congruence?

Zeller's Congruence is a mathematical algorithm developed by Christian Zeller to calculate the day of the week for any given date in the Gregorian or Julian calendars.

Why are January and February treated as months 13 and 14 of the previous year?

This adjustment simplifies the leap year calculation. By treating January and February as the end of the previous year, the leap day (February 29th) is always the last day of the calculation year, which makes the formula consistent.

Is this calculator accurate?

Yes, this calculator is highly accurate for any date since the adoption of the Gregorian calendar (October 15, 1582). It correctly handles the 10-day skip implemented during the calendar reform.
